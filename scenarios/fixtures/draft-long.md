# Long Draft

## Why This Exists

It is important to note that the onboarding process has become complicated over time. Each team added a little bit of helpful context, and the result is now a document that tries to explain everything at once. New employees read it when they are least prepared to distinguish between what matters today and what can wait.

The purpose of this draft is to create a shared view of what should happen in the first week. It does not need to answer every possible question. It needs to make the first few days feel legible, humane, and practical.

## What New Employees Need

New employees need to know who is responsible for them. That sounds obvious, but it is often distributed across a manager, a buddy, a recruiter, an operations partner, and a set of automated messages. When everyone owns a slice of the experience, no one owns the feeling of being welcomed.

They also need a clear map of the week. The current calendar includes benefits, compliance, security, product orientation, team rituals, and one-on-one meetings. Some of those sessions are necessary. Some are useful later. Some are legacy meetings that remain on the calendar because nobody has removed them.

Finally, they need permission to ask basic questions. A new person should not have to guess whether a question belongs in Slack, email, a ticket, a document comment, or a meeting. The first-week guide should name the channel for common questions and the person who will answer them.

## What Managers Need

Managers need a smaller job. The current process asks managers to coordinate every detail while also judging whether the new person is ramping well. That mix makes the manager both host and evaluator. The result is that the warm parts of onboarding are easy to drop when the manager is busy.

A better process gives managers a short checklist. Before day one, they confirm the role goals, the buddy, and the first meaningful project. During week one, they meet twice: once to orient, once to answer questions after the new person has seen the system. Everything else should be owned by the process, not by manager memory.

## What To Cut

We should cut duplicate introductions. The company overview, team overview, and product overview currently repeat the same origin story. Repetition can help, but this repetition does not build understanding. It makes the new person listen politely while waiting for the part that applies to their work.

We should cut meetings that only transmit links. If the meeting exists to tell someone where a document lives, the document should be linked in the checklist. Live time should be reserved for judgment, context, and questions that actually benefit from another person being present.

We should cut vague advice. Phrases like "be proactive," "ask good questions," and "get up to speed" sound helpful but do not tell anyone what to do. The guide should say what to read, who to meet, what to ship, and when to ask for help.

## The First Week

Day one should be simple. The new person should meet the manager, meet the buddy, get access to the necessary systems, and understand the first small project. The day should not be a scavenger hunt through tools and policy pages.

Day two should be about context. The new person reads the team charter, reviews recent work, and asks the buddy to explain the parts that do not make sense. The goal is not mastery. The goal is to build enough context to ask better questions.

Day three should include the first real contribution. It can be small, but it should be connected to actual team work. A tiny real task teaches more than a large fake exercise because it shows the new person how review, feedback, and deployment actually happen.

Day four should be reserved for follow-up. By then, the new person has hit enough friction to know what is confusing. The buddy and manager should expect questions and make space for them.

Day five should close the loop. The manager names what went well, what still feels unclear, and what the second week should focus on. The new person should leave the first week with a plan, not just a pile of links.
